The Blind Spot in Every Manufacturer's Budget

Why the second-largest line on the books is also the least understood — and what changes the moment you can finally ask it a question.

There is a number sitting inside almost every manufacturing company that nobody can fully explain.

It isn't the cost of raw materials, or salaries, or freight. Those get scrutinised line by line. It's the money spent on trade promotions — the endless schemes a company offers its distributors and dealers to push product down the channel. Buy a hundred bags, get five free. Hit your quarterly target, earn two percent back. Stock the new SKU, take an extra slab discount.

Individually these look small. Together they are enormous. In consumer goods, trade promotions routinely consume around a fifth of revenue — often the second-largest line on the books after the goods themselves. And here is the uncomfortable part: by most credible estimates, the majority of that spend doesn't even break even.

The strange thing isn't that promotions fail. It's that companies usually can't tell which ones did.

A promise, reconciled by hand

To understand why, you have to see a scheme for what it really is. It isn't a transaction. It's a conditional promise — "do this, and we'll reward you later" — that gets claimed weeks afterward and reconciled, painfully, by hand.

That single fact creates a fog. Did sales actually rise because of the scheme, or would those bags have sold anyway? Did a distributor genuinely earn the incentive they claimed, or quietly inflate it? Are three "small" discounts silently stacking on the same invoice into one large one nobody approved? Is a distributor buying aggressively because there's real demand, or just to bank the bonus and let stock pile up?

The data that could answer all of this exists. It's just scattered across sales, billing, claims and inventory systems that were never designed to talk to each other. Pulling it together takes weeks of spreadsheet work — and by the time the answer arrives, the next scheme cycle has already begun. So teams do the only thing they can: they design the next round on gut feel and last quarter's memory.

Systems that record, but never reason

We've spent decades getting very good at recording business. ERP systems capture every accrual, every billing document, every payout with perfect fidelity. But recording is not the same as understanding.

A transactional system will tell you, flawlessly, that a payout happened. It will not tell you whether it was worth it. It won't say which scheme to scale, which to kill, or which distributor to watch. That judgment — the leap from data to decision — has always been left to a human with a spreadsheet and not enough time.

That gap, between what the data knows and what a person can act on, is the whole problem. And it's exactly the gap that has suddenly become closeable.

From a dashboard to a decision

The instinct, when faced with messy data, is to build a dashboard. But a dashboard only ever tells you what happened. It still leaves the hardest work — interpreting it, prioritising it, deciding — to you.

The more interesting question is: what if you could simply ask?

That's the idea behind PromoLens AI. Not another report, but a copilot for trade promotions. One screen that opens not with a wall of charts but with a verdict: for every rupee you spent on schemes, here's what came back; here's the money leaking right now; here's your single biggest win to repeat and your one scheme that's quietly losing money — and beside it, a chat you can ask anything, in plain English, and get the number and the next move.

It collapses three weeks of reconciliation into a sentence. "Which scheme should I stop?" "Who's loading inventory?" "What should I launch next, and in which market?" The answers come back immediately, in the language a sales leader actually uses — not SQL, not a pivot table. And because promotions are judged over time, you can look at the rolling year — the benchmark the whole industry plans around — or step back into any past financial year and watch the entire picture recompute against the right baseline.

The principle that makes it trustworthy

Here is the part that matters most, and the part that's easy to get wrong in the age of generative AI. When a tool is reporting your ROI and flagging what looks like fraud, it cannot afford to be creative with numbers. So PromoLens draws a hard line: the AI explains; an engine proves.

The language model — Google's Gemini — is the interface. It understands the question, decides which analysis to run, and narrates the result like a sharp colleague. But the figures themselves never come from the model. They come from deterministic calculations over the company's own data, reproducible and auditable to the rupee. The intelligence you talk to is conversational; the intelligence you trust is accountable. Keeping those two things separate is what turns a clever demo into something a finance team would actually act on.

It's all built on Google Cloud — Gemini for the reasoning and the conversation, BigQuery holding the data, and a few well-chosen services doing the quiet work underneath. But the stack is not the story. The story is the shift it represents.

What actually changes

When analysis stops being a three-week project and becomes a thirty-second question, something subtle but profound happens to how a company behaves. Decisions stop waiting for the analyst. The person closest to the market — the regional sales head, the trade-marketing manager — can interrogate their own spend directly, the moment they wonder about it. Money that used to leak silently for six weeks gets caught on day one. And the institutional memory that normally lives in one veteran's head, and walks out the door when they leave, becomes something anyone can simply ask for.

This is the real promise of putting AI on top of your own data. Not replacing judgment — democratising it. Giving everyone who touches the business the ability to ask it honest questions and get honest answers.

The bigger picture

Trade promotion happens to be a near-perfect example of a problem that's everywhere: enormous spend, scattered data, decisions made on instinct because the truth is too slow to reach. Marketing budgets look like this. Supply chains look like this. So does most of the money a large company moves.

For a long time the bottleneck wasn't the data — it was the distance between the data and a decision. That distance is collapsing. The companies that win the next decade won't be the ones with the most dashboards. They'll be the ones whose people can simply ask — and trust the answer.

PromoLens AI is one small, concrete proof of that future, pointed at one expensive, overlooked corner of manufacturing. The blind spot doesn't have to stay blind.
